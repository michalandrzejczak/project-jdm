    • JDM

    Japanese domestic market 日本の国内市場

    JDM vehicles - meant to be driven in Japan and differ greatly from the cars that Japanese manufacturers build for export. The Japanese car owner looks more toward innovation than long-term ownership which forces Japanese carmakers to refine new technologies and designs first in domestic vehicles.

    Ftrs

    domestic != export //true

    In 1988, JDM cars were limited to 280 horsepower (PS) (276 hp) and a top speed of 180 km/h (111.8 mph) - limits imposed by the Japan Automobile Manufacturers Association (JAMA) for safety. Many JDM cars have speedometers that register up to 180 km/hb (certain Nissans go up to 190 km/h, and the GT-R has a mechanism that removes the speed limiter on a track) but all have speed limiters.

    Kanjo

    True JDM origin

    Kanjo is the loop of elevated section of the Hanshin Expressway that circles the center of Osaka. When Kanjo is filled with ordinary drivers, Civic fans gather, form relationships and race each other. Why old Honda Civic (produced to 2001)? It's race history on Suzuka Circuit, VTEC engine and small size made it great, simple street race car that was intentionally made for Japanese market.

    Ring

    Tsurikawa - JDM not only about cars

    Tsurikawas - “hang” (tsuri吊) and “leather” (kawa革) - train/subway handle rings stolen in trains by local young gangsters, also known as bosozokus. Originally attached inside cars so passengers could slide their wrist inside the ring and safely hang their body outside the window. They quickly became the symbol of a reckless youth and bosozokus started to attach them outside heavily modified bikes and cars to show rebellion and disrespect to authority. Nowadays, JDM enthusiasts are still flaunting them to express their freedom.
